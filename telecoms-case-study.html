Section 01

Executive Summary

This case study documents a data quality engagement conducted for a Nigerian telecommunications operator undergoing an ownership transition. As part of a six-person data quality team, I was personally responsible for profiling, cleansing, and analysing over four million subscriber records — contributing to an organisation-wide audit spanning approximately 20 million total subscribers.

The engagement identified 1.8 million duplicate subscriber accounts and approximately four million inactive subscribers, revealing that the operator's true active, revenue-generating base was materially smaller than the figures on record. These findings directly informed the acquisition valuation and exposed a significant credit recovery risk arising from an undercollateralised student loan scheme launched on an incomplete data foundation.

Beyond the audit, the team designed and delivered a customer data lifecycle framework, a revised KYC onboarding standard aligned to NCC regulatory requirements, and an enterprise-wide data stewardship programme — including in-house training for operational and management staff. A 30-day post-implementation monitoring period validated framework adoption.

Section 02

Business Context

The client was a mid-scale Nigerian mobile network operator with an approximate subscriber base of 20 million — the third-largest operator in one of Africa's most competitive telecoms markets. At the time of engagement, Nigeria's total mobile subscriptions stood at approximately 171.6 million.

The operator was undergoing its second acquisition in recent years. The incoming ownership required an independent assessment of operational and data health as part of due diligence. Data governance had not been a strategic priority during the prior transition, resulting in accumulated data quality debt across core systems — with no formal data ownership structure and inconsistent KYC standards across onboarding channels.

Why this context matters

An M&A due diligence setting is one of the highest-stakes environments for a data quality engagement. Findings do not merely inform operational improvements — they directly influence company valuation, deal structure, and investor confidence.

Active Subscriber Definition

A subscriber was classified as 'active' if they had recorded any network activity — calls, data usage, or financial transaction — within the preceding 180 days. This definition was agreed as a formal business rule before any analysis began and applied consistently throughout the engagement.

Section 04

Methodology

The engagement followed a structured four-phase approach: Assess → Diagnose → Remediate → Embed.

Phase 1 — Onboarding Pause & Baseline Assessment (Days 1–15)

New customer onboarding was placed on hold for the first 15 days. This significant operational decision — pausing the primary subscriber acquisition function — was justified by the scale of integrity issues identified at scoping. The pause established a clean, stable baseline without ongoing contamination from non-compliant new registrations.

Each data domain was profiled against the six standard dimensions of data quality:

DQ DimensionHow it Manifested
CompletenessKYC records missing mandatory PII fields; subscriber records with no contact data
Uniqueness1.8M duplicate accounts via PII matching across name, address, NIN, contact number
Accuracy600+ enterprise customers misclassified as individual — systematic underbilling
ValidityKYC records not meeting NCC standards; inconsistent formats across channels
Timeliness~4M subscribers classified inactive under the 180-day rule
ConsistencyDefinitions and standards differed between field agent and service centre channels

Phase 2 — Duplicate Detection & Record Matching

A multi-level matching strategy was applied to the full subscriber database using composite confidence scoring:

  • Name matching — exact and fuzzy matching to capture spelling variants common in Nigerian names (e.g. 'Mohammed' vs 'Muhammad'), supplemented by phonetic algorithms.
  • Address matching — normalised and matched with partial thresholds, acknowledging that Nigerian address formats are frequently inconsistent.
  • NIN matching — the most reliable single identifier where available; coverage was incomplete reflecting NIN-SIM linkage compliance at the time.
  • Contact number matching — MSISDNs and secondary numbers cross-matched; shared phone numbers across accounts are a strong duplicate signal.

Phase 3 — KYC Audit Against NCC Standards

KYC records were assessed against NCC subscriber registration requirements — the formal regulatory benchmark. Gaps were systematically linked to onboarding channel: field agent registrations consistently contained fewer PII fields than service centre registrations, a root cause finding rather than a symptom.

Section 05

Key Findings

5.1 Subscriber Base Integrity

CategoryCount% of Reported Base
Total Reported Subscribers~20,000,000100%
Confirmed Duplicates~1,800,0009.0%
Inactive Subscribers (180d)~4,000,00020.0%
True Active Base (estimated)~14,200,000~71%

The acquiring party assumed a 20 million subscriber base. The audit established the true active, non-duplicate count at approximately 14.2 million — a 29% reduction. This gap had a direct and material impact on the acquisition valuation.

5.2 KYC Compliance Gaps

Significant gaps were concentrated in field agent onboarding records, created under a 'minimum viable KYC' approach — sufficient to activate a SIM, but insufficient to meet regulatory standards or support downstream processes such as credit recovery.

5.3 CRM Misclassification

Over 600 accounts were misclassified as individual customers when they were enterprise accounts. Enterprise billing rates are materially higher; the accumulated underbilling represented meaningful revenue leakage and understated forward revenue projections for the acquiring party.

5.4 The Student Credit Scheme — A Case Study Within a Case Study

The operator had launched a student credit scheme offering short-term loans of up to ₦10,000 to eligible subscribers. The scheme was deployed on a subscriber base that lacked the data depth to support credit risk assessment or debt recovery. When defaults occurred, recovery proved near-impossible.

Minimum KYC standard at onboarding

Insufficient customer data captured at point of registration

Credit scheme launched on weak data foundation

Defaults occurred; recovery impossible without adequate PII

Financial auditors discounted debt recoverability in valuation

Acquisition valuation reduced

This causal chain — from data quality failure at point of onboarding to financial loss at board level — is one of the clearest illustrations of data quality risk available in practice.

Key Insight

These root causes are systemic, not accidental. Fixing the data without addressing underlying causes would have produced a clean dataset that degraded again within 12–18 months.

Section 07

Recommendations & Frameworks Delivered

7.1 Customer Data Lifecycle Framework

  • Standardised onboarding requirements — a single, channel-agnostic set of minimum PII fields across all onboarding routes.
  • Two-tier onboarding model — field agents retained rapid onboarding capability, but field-registered subscribers were required to complete full registration at a service centre within 90 days.
  • Active/inactive lifecycle transitions — formal rules governing state transitions and what actions each triggers.
  • Data retention standards — aligned to NCC regulatory requirements.

7.2 Revised KYC Standard

  • Mandatory NIN capture at registration (aligned to NCC NIN-SIM linkage policy)
  • Minimum address verification with structured format to enable matching
  • Secondary contact number capture above defined usage thresholds
  • Enterprise account identification criteria formalised in CRM workflow

7.3 Data Stewardship Structure (via Collibra)

  • Each data domain assigned a named business owner accountable for ongoing quality
  • Critical data elements — including 'active subscriber' — documented in a business glossary
  • Formal escalation path for raising and resolving data quality issues

7.4 Power BI Data Quality Monitoring Dashboard

Ongoing KPIs built into a live dashboard — duplicate rate, KYC completeness score, active/inactive ratio, CRM classification accuracy — enabling the client to track subscriber data health post-engagement.

Section 08

Implementation & Monitoring

Following framework delivery, the team remained engaged for a 30-day monitoring period to validate adoption and measure early performance against the new quality KPIs. Frameworks that are designed but not monitored frequently revert to prior behaviour within weeks — this period was critical.

Enterprise-wide training was delivered in Nigeria covering: NCC regulatory requirements, the current state scorecard, 90-day and 12-month forward projections, enterprise-wide data stewardship responsibilities, and KYC implementation guidance for operational staff. I contributed to programme design and content development.

Section 10

Lessons Learned

What Worked Well

  • Defining business rules before touching data — the active/inactive definition prevented contradictory outputs and gave all stakeholders a shared reference point.
  • The two-tier onboarding model — preserving field agent speed while building in a compliance step was the right balance of governance and practicality.
  • Root cause orientation — framing findings around systemic causes meant recommendations addressed durable change, not one-off cleansing.

What Could Be Strengthened

  • Deeper probabilistic matching — incorporating IMEI, date of birth, and email would have surfaced additional duplicates and reduced manual review burden.
  • Earlier stakeholder alignment — tighter upfront scoping documentation would have reduced scope renegotiation mid-engagement.
  • Post-training measurement — formal KYC completion rate tracking at 30 and 90 days post-training would have provided stronger impact evidence.
The Broader Lesson

Data quality is not a technical problem. The root causes of every issue in this engagement were organisational — ownership gaps, cultural priorities, process inconsistencies, and inherited decisions. The real intervention was at the level of governance: establishing accountability, standards, and the expectation that data health is a business responsibility, not an IT afterthought.
