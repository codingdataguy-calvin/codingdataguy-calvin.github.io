Section 01

Executive Summary

This case study documents 26 months of embedded data stewardship at Guaranty Trust Bank Plc (GTCO Plc), one of Nigeria's foremost tier-one commercial banks, listed on both the Nigerian Exchange Group and the London Stock Exchange. As Data Steward II within the Commercial Banking Group, I was responsible for the data quality, integrity, governance, and regulatory compliance of customer data across a regional portfolio spanning 52 branches and approximately 3.9 million customers.

The role covered two distinct but interconnected workstreams. The first was ongoing stewardship: maintaining a single source of truth across marketing and commercial data domains through the Business Objects reporting platform, managing PII access governance, and ensuring regulatory compliance with CBN, NDPC, and AML requirements daily. The second was a major programme contribution: the bank's migration from its legacy core banking system, Basis, to the Infosys Finacle platform — a 90-day, three-batch cutover.

Across the migration workstream, I assessed 7,000 customer accounts for CIF consolidation, of which 2,000+ required full re-profiling. These contributed to the consolidation of over 100,000 duplicate accounts into single Customer Information Files across the regional portfolio. A critical gap was identified: 63% of corporate accounts in scope had been captured under the legacy system by CAC registration number alone, without the identity validation data required by Finacle.

The NIN and BVN linkage programme produced a step-change result: coverage increased from 9% over 12 months to 33% within 60 days. During the migration classification work, a portfolio of non-performing loan balances stale for over ten years was identified with a combined book value of approximately ₦4.2 billion naira. The executive team proceeded with write-offs of ₦3.8 billion, directly supported by the stewardship function's classification and documentation work.

Core Proposition

This engagement demonstrates what embedded data stewardship looks like in practice inside a regulated financial institution — not as a one-off intervention, but as a sustained discipline that makes high-stakes programmes like core banking migrations possible.

Section 02

About the Institution & Market Context

Guaranty Trust Bank Plc, operating under the holding company GTCO Plc, is one of Nigeria's most recognised tier-one commercial banks. Founded in 1990 and headquartered in Lagos, GTCO Plc is listed on both the Nigerian Exchange Group (NGX) and the London Stock Exchange (LSE) — making it one of a small number of African financial institutions with a dual listing and direct exposure to UK regulatory expectations.

The regional desk to which I was assigned covered 52 branches, with a total customer base of approximately 3.9 million across retail and commercial segments. My stewardship remit covered the marketing and commercial banking data domains, encompassing customer onboarding records, KYC data, product holdings, consent flags, and loan and overdraft account data.

Section 03

Background & Role Context

Prior to joining Guaranty Trust Bank, I worked as a Data Analyst — building the technical foundation in SQL, data profiling, and reporting that underpinned the governance work. My entry into GTCO was not directly into a data function. I joined as a Commercial Banking Officer, a client-facing role in which I worked directly with corporate and commercial customers, supported relationship managers, and developed a working understanding of banking products, credit facilities, overdraft structures, and the commercial customer lifecycle.

This year in commercial banking was not incidental to my later data work. It was the foundation of it. When I later had to classify loan portfolios, assess corporate account completeness, or escalate ambiguous CIF consolidations to account managers, I was not operating as an outsider interpreting business data. I was a practitioner who understood what that data represented in commercial reality.

The Move Into Data Stewardship

After a year in commercial banking, I moved into the role of Data Steward II through an internal process. The transition was natural: I had already demonstrated the business context and analytical background the role required, and I had actively sought the opportunity when it became available. The role sat within a governance structure reporting to a Senior Enterprise Data Architect and Compliance supervisors — held to architectural standards on one side and regulatory standards on the other.

Section 04

The Day-to-Day Stewardship Role

One of the distinctions between project-based data quality work and embedded stewardship is that the latter has no finish line. The daily and weekly routines described here reflect the steady-state operating model that ran before, alongside, and after the migration programme. During the 90-day migration period, profiling, classification, and validation took priority — but the governance baseline described here remained the foundation throughout.

Morning Data Quality Reviews

Each working day began with a review of the Data Quality dashboard — a branch-level monitoring system updated on a five-day cycle. The review covered automated alerts, anomalies in records used for daily liquidity reporting, and datasets that needed to be clean and locked before feeding downstream processes. A minimum of four data quality anomalies were identified and resolved per week through this process.

Golden Source Enforcement

I was responsible for ensuring that the Business Objects reporting platform functioned as the primary and exclusive source of truth for all marketing and commercial data consumption. This required active enforcement: directing all data requests through the BO platform, monitoring for unofficial extracts, and working with data engineers to incorporate new data requirements into the platform rather than through workarounds. The BO platform sat on top of the SQL database, with Power BI used for dashboard visualisation and stakeholder reporting.

PII Access Governance

Managing access to Personally Identifiable Information was a daily operational responsibility. Under the NDPA 2023, access to customer PII must be proportionate, purposeful, and documented. Access controls determined which users could see which data and at what level of granularity, ensuring sensitive customer information reached only those with a legitimate business need and the appropriate authorisation.

Data Lineage and Catalogue

Seven datasets were formally tagged and documented within the data catalogue during the tenure, covering the core customer, account, and product data assets. Each dataset was documented with its source, lineage, update frequency, access permissions, and business definition.

Section 05

The Basis-to-Finacle Core Banking Migration

The most significant programme contribution of the tenure was the bank's migration from its legacy core banking platform, Basis, to the Infosys Finacle system. The migration was structured as a 90-day programme executed in three batches. My contribution covered the marketing and commercial banking data domains, with direct responsibility for assessing 7,000 customer accounts for CIF consolidation and migration readiness.

5.1 Pre-Migration Data Health Assessment

Of the 7,000 accounts assessed, 2,000+ required full re-profiling across four dimensions: completeness, accuracy, validity, and documentation completeness. A significant structural finding emerged: 63% of corporate accounts in scope had been captured under Basis using only their CAC registration number, without associated identity validation data — director IDs, ID validity dates, or supporting documentation required by Finacle as a mandatory field.

5.2 The CIF Consolidation

Across the regional portfolio, over 100,000 accounts were identified as duplicates requiring consolidation under single Customer Information Files. My direct contribution covered 7,000 of those accounts. Where records were ambiguous, they were escalated to the account manager for clarification. Records that could not be resolved were flagged and reported to the AML and compliance desk. In a regulated environment, an incorrect consolidation is a potential compliance failure, not a minor data error.

5.3 Data Remediation

Tax ID records were reviewed and updated across the portfolio. Overdraft and loan accounts were classified using a predefined rule set covering six dimensions: origination date, facility type, product category, lifecycle stage, condition, and performance status. The predefined rule set was the quality control mechanism — ensuring consistent outputs across a complex, distributed portfolio where judgement alone would have introduced variation.

5.4 Marketing Consent Management

The consent programme was coordinated across all 52 branches, with account officers responsible for direct customer contact. The stewardship function's role was oversight and data governance rather than direct delivery: tracking opt-in rates at branch level, maintaining the data infrastructure recording consent flags accurately, ensuring the default treatment for non-respondents was correctly implemented, and reporting progress to the governance team. The programme achieved over 90% opt-in compliance.

Data Integrity Point

Every consent decision — whether explicit or default — was accurately recorded, auditable, and aligned with NDPA requirements. In a 52-branch regional portfolio, the integrity of that data record was as important as the compliance rate itself.

Section 06

The Non-Performing Loan Portfolio

The loan and overdraft classification work described in Section 5 was designed to prepare facilities data for migration. What it also did — as a direct by-product of the structured profiling exercise — was surface a subset of accounts that had no place in a clean core banking environment. During the process of batching facilities, a portfolio of non-performing loan balances emerged with a combined book value of approximately ₦4.2 billion naira — stale for over ten years, with no recovery activity on record.

These loan balances had accumulated, unresolved, across more than a decade, carried on the books through prior ownership transitions and a previous system migration without being identified or addressed. The migration profiling exercise, by forcing every facility through a structured classification process for the first time, made them visible.

The CBN Recapitalisation Context

The timing was commercially significant. The CBN's recapitalisation directive was active, requiring Nigerian banks to meet revised minimum capital thresholds. The executive team proceeded with write-offs totalling ₦3.8 billion naira — the portion assessed as having no realistic recovery value. The remaining ₦400 million was retained with active recovery measures attached.

Why This Matters

A steward working through a routine migration classification exercise identified a portfolio with direct balance sheet implications, surfaced it through the correct governance channel, and provided the evidence base for an executive decision that supported the bank's capital position during a period of regulatory scrutiny. That is what embedded stewardship looks like when it is functioning correctly.

Section 07

The Regulatory Dimension

At GTCO Plc, the compliance thread ran through every aspect of the stewardship role. The NIN-BVN linkage programme produced a step-change result: starting coverage was 9% after 12 months of previous effort. Within 60 days, linkage coverage reached 33% — an improvement achieved through a structured, systematic approach to identifying and resolving gaps across the regional portfolio.

The NDPA 2023 introduced binding requirements around consent management, data minimisation, and PII access governance that directly shaped both the consent programme and the BO access control architecture. AML obligations governed by CBN guidelines required Enhanced Due Diligence flagging for foreign national and expatriate accounts, and escalation of ambiguous CIF consolidation cases to the compliance desk. Data stewardship enables AML compliance. It does not replace it.

Section 09

Lessons Learned & Reflections

A case study that only records successes is a marketing document, not a professional one. The reflections below are genuine.

What Worked Well

  • The commercial banking background — walking into conversations with relationship managers with direct experience of their work made the governance work credible to the people it depended on.
  • The predefined rule set for loan classification — consistency at scale requires rules, not judgement. The quality of the migration output was directly dependent on the quality of the rules that governed it.
  • The escalation discipline — escalating ambiguous CIF consolidations first to account managers, then to the AML desk where needed, produced clean outcomes and maintained compliance integrity.
  • The default treatment design — applying the most privacy-preserving compliant default for non-consenting customers resolved a potential compliance gap before it became one.

What Could Be Strengthened

  • Post-migration monitoring — a deliberately designed monitoring period with defined KPIs tracked against the pre-migration baseline would have provided stronger evidence of whether the remediation work held in the new system over time.
  • Earlier engagement with account managers — many ambiguous CIF cases could have been pre-empted by accessing their institutional knowledge earlier in the process.
  • Post-programme measurement — formal assessment of branch officer understanding of the consent requirement would have provided stronger evidence of the programme's long-term sustainability.
The Broader Lesson

Twenty-six months of embedded stewardship at a tier-one bank taught me that the value of good data governance is not visible in a single deliverable. It accumulates quietly, over time, in the quality of decisions made on clean data, in the compliance gaps that never become incidents, and in the migration programmes that go smoothly because the data foundation was solid going in. Data stewardship is fundamentally a people discipline. The technical tools are the instruments. The work that actually moves the needle is the conversation — with the relationship manager, the branch officer, the compliance supervisor. The steward's job is to bridge those gaps. That bridging function is what I brought to GTCO, and it is what I carry forward.
